# Glioma and Glioblastoma: Clinical Overview

## What Is a Glioma?

A glioma is a type of brain tumor that originates in the glial cells — the supportive cells of the central nervous system. Glial cells include astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and ependymal cells. Gliomas are the most common type of primary malignant brain tumor in adults, accounting for approximately 80% of malignant brain tumors.

Gliomas are classified using the World Health Organization (WHO) grading system from Grade 1 (slowest growing, most favorable outcome) to Grade 4 (fastest growing, most aggressive). Grades 1 and 2 are considered low-grade; Grades 3 and 4 are high-grade.

## Glioblastoma (GBM) — WHO Grade 4

Glioblastoma multiforme, now simply called glioblastoma (GBM), is the most common and most aggressive primary malignant brain tumor in adults. It is a Grade 4 astrocytoma — meaning it arises from astrocyte cells and has the fastest growth rate.

GBM accounts for about 14.6% of all primary brain tumors and about 48.6% of all malignant primary brain tumors. The median age at diagnosis is 64 years. GBM is slightly more common in men than women.

## Subtypes Based on Molecular Markers

Modern glioma classification relies heavily on molecular markers, not just microscopic appearance.

**IDH Status:** The IDH1 or IDH2 gene mutation is one of the most important prognostic markers. IDH-mutant gliomas tend to occur in younger patients and have a better prognosis. IDH-wildtype glioblastoma is the most aggressive form.

**MGMT Methylation:** MGMT is a DNA repair enzyme. When the MGMT gene promoter is methylated, the enzyme is silenced, making tumor cells more vulnerable to chemotherapy (temozolomide). MGMT methylation is a positive prognostic and predictive marker — patients whose tumors are MGMT-methylated typically respond better to treatment.

**1p/19q Co-deletion:** This chromosomal marker is characteristic of oligodendroglioma and is associated with a better prognosis and better response to chemotherapy.

## Symptoms of Glioma

Symptoms depend on the location and size of the tumor and may include:

- Persistent or worsening headaches (often worse in the morning)
- Seizures (new-onset seizures in an adult with no prior history should prompt imaging)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Cognitive changes: memory loss, confusion, personality changes
- Focal neurological deficits: weakness on one side of the body, speech difficulties, visual changes
- Fatigue and general decline

Symptoms often develop gradually for low-grade gliomas but can appear suddenly in high-grade tumors.

## MRI Appearance of Glioma

On MRI, gliomas have characteristic appearances:

- **Low-grade gliomas (Grade 2):** Hypointense on T1, hyperintense on T2/FLAIR, typically no contrast enhancement, poorly defined borders, minimal surrounding edema.
- **High-grade gliomas (Grade 3–4):** Heterogeneous signal, ring-enhancing lesion on contrast T1, surrounding vasogenic edema (bright halo on T2/FLAIR), central necrosis (dark center on T1), mass effect (midline shift).

Ring enhancement — a bright ring on contrast-enhanced T1 MRI surrounding a dark necrotic core — is the classic imaging signature of glioblastoma.

## Treatment of Glioma

**Surgery:** The primary treatment goal is maximal safe resection — removing as much tumor as possible while preserving neurological function. Complete resection is rarely possible because glioma cells infiltrate surrounding normal brain tissue.

**Radiation Therapy:** Standard treatment for high-grade gliomas involves 6 weeks of external beam radiation therapy (60 Gy in 30 fractions) to the tumor bed with a surrounding margin.

**Chemotherapy:** Temozolomide (TMZ) is the standard chemotherapy agent for GBM, given concurrently with radiation and then as adjuvant therapy. Bevacizumab (Avastin), a VEGF inhibitor, is used for recurrent GBM to reduce tumor vascularity and control edema.

The standard of care for newly diagnosed GBM is the Stupp protocol: surgery followed by concurrent radiation and temozolomide, then 6 cycles of adjuvant temozolomide.

## Prognosis

Prognosis depends strongly on grade and molecular markers.

- **Grade 2 IDH-mutant:** Median survival of 5–15 years
- **Grade 3 IDH-mutant:** Median survival of 3–5 years
- **Grade 4 (GBM) IDH-wildtype:** Median survival of 14–16 months with standard treatment; 5-year survival rate is less than 10%

GBM almost always recurs after treatment. Recurrence is managed with re-resection, repeat radiation (if feasible), or second-line chemotherapy. Clinical trials are an important option at recurrence.

## Important Note for Patients

A classification result of "glioma" from an MRI classification AI is not a diagnosis. It indicates that the scan pattern shares visual features with glioma cases in the training data. Definitive diagnosis requires formal radiological review, biopsy, and histopathological analysis by a neuropathologist. Always consult a neurosurgeon or neuro-oncologist for proper evaluation.
