# Meningioma: Clinical Overview

## What Is a Meningioma?

A meningioma is a tumor that arises from the meninges — the three layers of membrane (dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater) that surround and protect the brain and spinal cord. Meningiomas are the most common primary brain tumor overall, accounting for approximately 37% of all primary brain and central nervous system tumors.

Despite being classified as a brain tumor, meningiomas are extra-axial — they grow from the meningeal lining rather than from brain tissue itself. They compress the brain from outside rather than infiltrating it.

## Who Gets Meningioma?

Meningiomas are significantly more common in women than men, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 2:1. They are most commonly diagnosed in adults between 40 and 70 years of age. Risk factors include:

- **Prior radiation exposure to the head** — the strongest established risk factor
- **Hormone exposure** — the higher rate in women, association with progesterone receptors, and rare relationship with breast cancer suggest a hormonal influence
- **Neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2)** — a genetic syndrome associated with multiple meningiomas
- **Obesity**

## WHO Grading of Meningioma

Meningiomas are graded 1 through 3 under the WHO classification:

**Grade 1 (Benign):** The vast majority — approximately 80% — are Grade 1. They grow slowly, are typically well-defined, and have low rates of recurrence after surgery. Most patients with Grade 1 meningiomas can be cured with complete surgical resection.

**Grade 2 (Atypical):** About 15–20% are atypical. They grow faster than Grade 1, have a higher recurrence rate, and often require post-surgical radiation.

**Grade 3 (Anaplastic/Malignant):** About 1–3% are malignant. These are aggressive, have high recurrence rates, and require aggressive treatment with surgery and radiation.

## Symptoms of Meningioma

Because meningiomas grow slowly, many are discovered incidentally on imaging done for another reason (e.g., a headache workup or trauma scan) and may be completely asymptomatic. When symptoms do occur, they depend on the tumor's location:

- **Convexity (top of brain):** Contralateral weakness or numbness, seizures
- **Parasagittal/falx:** Leg weakness, bladder dysfunction
- **Sphenoid wing:** Visual changes, eye movement problems
- **Olfactory groove:** Loss of smell, behavioral changes, cognitive decline
- **Posterior fossa:** Balance problems, hearing loss, cranial nerve deficits
- **General:** Headache (due to mass effect), increased intracranial pressure

## MRI Appearance of Meningioma

Meningiomas have a classic and often unmistakable appearance on MRI:

- **Location:** Extra-axial (outside brain parenchyma), attached to the dura
- **T1:** Isointense or slightly hypointense to gray matter
- **T2:** Variable — often iso- to hyperintense
- **Contrast enhancement:** Intense, homogeneous enhancement is the hallmark — meningiomas are among the most brightly enhancing tumors
- **Dural tail sign:** A linear enhancement along the dura adjacent to the tumor — highly characteristic of meningioma
- **Calcification:** Common in benign meningiomas — appears as dark areas on MRI, bright on CT
- **Mass effect:** Compression of adjacent brain, gyral buckling

The combination of extra-axial location, intense homogeneous enhancement, dural tail, and a broad dural base is diagnostic for meningioma in most cases.

## Treatment of Meningioma

**Watch and Wait:** Asymptomatic, small (< 3 cm), incidentally discovered meningiomas in older patients are often managed with periodic MRI surveillance, typically every 6–12 months initially. Many never require treatment.

**Surgery:** The primary treatment for symptomatic or growing meningiomas. The goal is Simpson Grade 1 resection — complete removal of the tumor and its dural attachment. Resectability depends on location; skull base meningiomas near critical vessels or nerves may only be partially resectable.

**Radiation Therapy:** Stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS, e.g., Gamma Knife, CyberKnife) is used for small to medium tumors or residual disease after surgery. Conventionally fractionated radiation is used for larger or higher-grade tumors.

**Medical therapy:** No systemic chemotherapy has proven effective for meningioma. Hydroxyurea has been used with modest results. Clinical trials are ongoing for atypical and anaplastic meningiomas.

## Prognosis

- **Grade 1:** Excellent. 5-year recurrence rate is 7–25% after gross total resection, 25–50% after subtotal resection. Overall survival is similar to age-matched population.
- **Grade 2:** 5-year recurrence rates of 29–52%. Radiation after surgery improves local control.
- **Grade 3:** Poor. 5-year recurrence rate exceeds 50%. Median survival 2–3 years.

## Important Note for Patients

A classification result of "meningioma" from an AI-based MRI classifier indicates visual similarity to meningioma patterns in the training data. It is not a diagnosis. Formal diagnosis requires review by a board-certified radiologist and, in most cases, histopathological confirmation. The good news is that most meningiomas are benign and many can be managed conservatively. Please consult a neurosurgeon for proper evaluation.
