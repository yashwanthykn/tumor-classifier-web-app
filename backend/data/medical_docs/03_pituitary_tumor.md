# Pituitary Tumors: Clinical Overview

## What Is a Pituitary Tumor?

A pituitary tumor (also called a pituitary adenoma) is a benign tumor that arises from the cells of the pituitary gland — a small, pea-sized gland at the base of the brain, sitting in a bony cavity called the sella turcica. The pituitary is often called the "master gland" because it regulates virtually all other endocrine glands in the body through hormone secretion.

Pituitary adenomas are among the most common intracranial tumors, accounting for approximately 10–15% of all primary brain tumors. Their true prevalence may be much higher — autopsy and radiological studies suggest up to 20% of the general population may harbor small asymptomatic pituitary adenomas.

The vast majority of pituitary adenomas are benign. Pituitary carcinomas (truly malignant) are exceedingly rare.

## Classification

### By Size
- **Microadenoma:** < 10 mm in diameter. Usually contained within the sella turcica.
- **Macroadenoma:** ≥ 10 mm. May extend above the sella turcica (suprasellar extension) and compress adjacent structures.
- **Giant adenoma:** > 40 mm. Rare; may cause significant mass effect.

### By Hormone Secretion
**Functioning (secreting) adenomas** produce excess hormone:

- **Prolactinoma:** Most common (about 40% of pituitary adenomas). Secretes prolactin. Causes galactorrhea (milk production outside of pregnancy/nursing) and amenorrhea in women; decreased libido and erectile dysfunction in men. Often responds to medical therapy (dopamine agonists) without surgery.
- **Growth hormone (GH)-secreting adenoma:** Causes acromegaly in adults (enlarged hands, feet, jaw, coarse facial features) or gigantism in children/adolescents.
- **ACTH-secreting adenoma:** Causes Cushing's disease — cortisol excess leading to central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, stretch marks, hypertension, diabetes, and immune suppression.
- **TSH-secreting adenoma:** Rare. Causes hyperthyroidism.
- **Gonadotroph adenoma:** Secretes LH/FSH; often clinically non-functioning.

**Non-functioning adenomas** do not secrete active hormone and are typically discovered because of mass effect symptoms.

## Symptoms

### Mass Effect Symptoms (especially macroadenomas)
- **Visual changes:** The optic chiasm sits directly above the pituitary gland. Suprasellar extension compresses the chiasm, causing bitemporal hemianopsia — loss of the outer (temporal) visual fields in both eyes. This is a classic finding that should prompt pituitary imaging.
- **Headache:** Due to stretching of the dura or increased intracranial pressure
- **Cranial nerve palsies:** Lateral extension into the cavernous sinus can compress cranial nerves III, IV, V1, V2, and VI, causing double vision or facial numbness.
- **Hypopituitarism:** Large tumors may compress normal pituitary tissue, causing deficiencies of GH, TSH, ACTH, LH/FSH, and antidiuretic hormone (diabetes insipidus).

### Hormone Excess Symptoms (functioning adenomas)
As described above depending on the secreted hormone.

### Pituitary Apoplexy
A rare emergency: sudden hemorrhage or infarction (death) of the pituitary adenoma. Presents with sudden severe headache ("thunderclap"), visual loss, and eye movement abnormalities. Requires urgent neurosurgical evaluation.

## MRI Appearance of Pituitary Adenoma

The pituitary gland is best visualized on MRI with thin coronal and sagittal slices through the sella turcica:

- **Microadenoma:** Often hypointense (darker than surrounding normal pituitary tissue) on T1 post-contrast. The normal pituitary enhances brightly with contrast; the adenoma enhances more slowly, appearing as a hypointense "hole" in the bright gland. May cause subtle asymmetry or upward bulging of the gland.
- **Macroadenoma:** Isointense to brain on T1, iso- to hyperintense on T2. Enhances heterogeneously with contrast. Key features: expansion of the sella, suprasellar extension (snowman or figure-8 shape), possible cavernous sinus invasion.
- **Stalk deviation:** The pituitary stalk may be pushed to the contralateral side by a microadenoma.
- **Hemorrhage/cyst:** Common within larger adenomas — creates heterogeneous signal on both T1 and T2.

## Treatment

**Prolactinoma:** First-line treatment is medical therapy with dopamine agonists (cabergoline or bromocriptine), which normalize prolactin levels and often shrink the tumor significantly. Surgery is reserved for drug-intolerant patients or rapidly worsening vision.

**Other functioning adenomas (GH, ACTH):** Transsphenoidal surgery (through the nose and sphenoid sinus, not requiring craniotomy) is the primary treatment. This minimally invasive approach allows direct access to the sella.

**Non-functioning macroadenomas:** Surgery is indicated for visual compromise, rapid growth, or significant mass effect. Small, asymptomatic non-functioning adenomas may be monitored with serial MRI.

**Radiation:** Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) or fractionated radiation is used for residual or recurrent disease after surgery, or when surgery is not possible.

**Medical therapy post-surgery:** Octreotide (somatostatin analog) for residual/recurrent GH-secreting tumors; pasireotide for Cushing's disease.

## Prognosis

Pituitary adenomas are benign in the vast majority of cases. Surgical remission rates for microadenomas are high (70–80% for Cushing's, 60–80% for acromegaly). Recurrence is possible, particularly with macroadenomas and incomplete resection. Lifelong follow-up with endocrinology and periodic MRI is standard.

## Important Note for Patients

An AI classification result of "pituitary tumor" indicates that the scan has features visually similar to pituitary adenoma patterns in the training dataset. This is not a diagnosis. Pituitary adenomas are almost always benign, and many are found incidentally and require no treatment. However, proper evaluation by an endocrinologist and neurosurgeon is essential to determine whether hormone excess or mass effect is present and whether treatment is needed.
